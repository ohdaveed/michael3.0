Important Estate Documents Everybody Needs

A complete estate plan is more than a single document — it's a coordinated set of legal instruments that work together to protect you, your assets, and the people you love.

01 — Core Document

Living Trust

Avoid the expense, delay, and public nature of probate if your gross estate exceeds $184,500. The minimum cost of probate in California is $5,980 — more than twice the cost of a Living Trust. A Living Trust allows your Successor Trustees to manage your assets for your benefit and the benefit of your loved ones, according to your own detailed instructions, if you become disabled and after you're gone — without the need, expense, public exposure, or delay of going to court.

You will need to name two Successor Trustees.
02 — Included with Trust

Pour Over Will

Accompanies all Revocable Trusts and includes appointing guardians for your minor children. Pour Over Wills also allow your Trustees to put omitted assets into the trust after the Trustmaker's death — ensuring nothing falls through the cracks.

You will need to name Guardians for your children and two Executors.
03 — Included with Trust

General Durable Power of Attorney

Permits your Agents to manage your personal matters — like paying bills and arranging for care — according to your written directions if you can no longer manage these duties on your own. This ensures your affairs continue to be handled without court involvement.

You will need to name two alternate Agents.
04 — Included with Trust

Advance Health Care Directive

Lets you direct exactly what kind of medical care decisions your Healthcare Agents will make on your behalf if you can no longer make these decisions for yourself — without court involvement. Your wishes are documented clearly and legally binding.

You will need to name two alternate Agents for Health Care.
05 — Included with Trust

HIPAA Authorizations

Allows your Healthcare Agents to communicate with your doctors, insurance companies, and other medical providers such as nurses and therapists. Without a HIPAA Authorization, your agents may be unable to access the medical information they need to advocate on your behalf.

You will need to name everyone you wish to have access to your medical information — at minimum, the agents on your Advance Health Care Directive.
